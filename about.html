FILED: A Blog for the History of Paperwork

Recovering the utopias and visions that animated paperwork, as well as now forgotten uses for it

About this blog

Founded in 2020, this blog brings together for the first time scholarship on paperwork – all documents produced in response to a demand (real or imagined) by an office, the law, a lab, or similar organizational entity. We aim to develop this into a high-quality collaborative, international, and peer-reviewed blog. Focus is on "paper revolution" (Delphine Gardey) 1800-1940 and we envision this to be less about topics covered by art and literary history than to facilitate deeper discussions among librarians, historians, curators, and archivists around all aspects of pre-electric data management and material cultures involved. Overlaps exist, of course, and are encouraged, also in terms of historical period, but the focus should be on 19th and 20th-centuries mass-scale information systems in private and public bureaucracies, archives, labs and similar data-gathering faciliities. Posts that tie historical scholarship to present-day issues related to inscriptions, reading, power, and information are particularly welcome.

Editorial Team

Managing Editor and Contributor
Jochen F Mayer

Jochen studied sociology and politics at universities in Berlin and Toulouse before completing his doctorate in history at the University of Edinburgh in 2012. He is an independent historian of 20th-century German state and public administration working at the interface between administrative history and the history of science and technology. He is currently preparing a book manuscript based on my PhD dissertation which takes the sprawling and (during Nazism) criminal labour administration as a case study to draw out some broader hypotheses on the relationships between state-building, information management, and bureaucratic authority from the late Weimar Republic, over the Nazi regime and Allied occupation, to 1960s West Germany. Working title is "Paper War: Office Work and the Government of Labour in Germany (1920-1960)." Jochen tweets from @jochenfm

Contributors and Advisors
James Purdon

James studied English at Emmanuel College, Cambridge, was a Herchel Smith scholar at Harvard University, and worked as a parliamentary reporter before completing his doctorate in 2012. James researches the intersection of literary narrative with technological and political systems over the course of the twentieth century, and has particular interests in modernist studies, Cold War-era culture, and contemporary fiction. He wrote Modernist Informatics (OUP, 2016) to understand how the idea of 'information' was understood as an aesthetic concept in early C20th British writing; now he is interested mostly in how technology, science, policy, rhetoric, and imaginative writing interact in 1950s and 1960s Britain and the British Empire/Commonwealth. James tweets from @infomodernist

Chris A Williams

Chris is a Senior Lecturer in History at the Open University, based in Milton Keynes, UK. His research interests centre around the history of policing in the UK and touch on broader related issues of state power. His book Police Control Systems in Britain, 1775-1975: from Parish Constable to National Computer was published by Manchester University Press, and he has also written on the framing of criminal statistics and the history of index-keeping as they influenced nineteenth century government. He is currently working on a chapter for the New Cambridge History of Britain on 'the regulated citizen'. Chris tweets from @OU_Williams
