About this blog

Founded in 2020... the Collaborative blog on “paper revolution” (Delphine Gardey) 1800-1940 // bringing together scholarship on paperwork oriented toward practical, rather than literary ends – all documents produced in response to a demand (real or imagined) by an office, the law, a lab, or similar organizational entity // to facilitate deeper discussions among librarians, historians, curators, & archivists around all aspects of pre-electric data management & material cultures involved // to form research network and prepare larger funding bid // help consolidate history of paperwork as an identifiable subfield.

Editorial Team

Managing Editor and Contributor
Jochen F Mayer

Jochen studied sociology and politics at universities in Berlin and Toulouse before completing his doctorate in history at the University of Edinburgh in 2012. He is an independent historian of 20th-century German state and public administration working at the interface between administrative history and the history of science and technology. He is currently preparing a book manuscript based on my PhD dissertation which takes the sprawling and (during Nazism) criminal labour administration as a case study to draw out some broader hypotheses on the relationships between state-building, information management, and bureaucratic authority from the late Weimar Republic, over the Nazi regime and Allied occupation, to 1960s West Germany. Working title is “Paper War: Office Work and the Government of Labour in Germany (1920-1960).” Jochen tweets from @jochenfm

Contributors and Advisors
James Purdon

James Purdon studied English at Emmanuel College, Cambridge, was a Herchel Smith scholar at Harvard University, and worked as a parliamentary reporter before completing his doctorate in 2012. James researches the intersection of literary narrative with technological and political systems over the course of the twentieth century, and has particular interests in modernist studies, Cold War-era culture, and contemporary fiction. He wrote Modernist Informatics (OUP, 2016) to understand how the idea of 'information' was understood as an aesthetic concept in early C20th British writing; now he is interested mostly in how technology, science, policy, rhetoric, and imaginative writing interact in 1950s and 1960s Britain and the British Empire/Commonwealth. James tweets from @infomodernist

Chris A Williams

Chris is a Senior Lecturer in History at Open University and is affiliated to the Centre for the History of Crime, Policing and Justice; and the Harm and Evidence Reseach Collective. He has an MA in Urban History from the University of Leicester, and a PhD from the University of Sheffield, with the thesis topic 'Police and Crime in Sheffield, 1818-1873'. His research focuses on the history of police bureacracy and its place within other police information systems, especially in the context of broader development of techniques of governement and associated reflexive use of knowledge within government. Chris tweets from @OU_Williams
